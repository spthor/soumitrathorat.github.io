PNSF FUNDED RESEARCH · GEORGE WASHINGTON UNIVERSITY

During COVID-19, millions of people held funerals, memorials, and grief rituals on video platforms never designed for any of it. Families were troubleshooting tech while grieving. I led a multi-method research program to understand what made virtual ceremonies feel meaningful and what got in the way. What we found challenged how platforms were framing the problem: users didn't want more flexibility. They wanted structure, presence, and less to figure out.

Stakeholders Mourners, funeral directors, clergy, community organizers
Communities African American, Jewish, Muslim, Catholic, Hindu
Platforms Zoom, Facebook Live, YouTube Live, Webex, Google Meet
Methods Interviews, video observation, diary studies, usability testing, heuristic evaluation

The Research Context

Video platforms were built for meetings. When COVID hit, they became the default infrastructure for one of the most emotionally significant things people do — say goodbye. Platform teams knew something wasn't working, but they didn't have the research to understand what or why.

We built the research program around those constraints. The first challenge was access: people needed to trust us before they'd talk about experiences that were still raw. I started with focus groups to build relationships before asking anyone to go deeper. That groundwork was what made the rest of the study possible.

The second challenge was reliability. Self-reported accounts of grief are incomplete as people process difficult experiences differently in reflection than they did in the moment. I needed to triangulate: between what participants told me in interviews, what I could observe in recorded ceremonies, and how their perspectives shifted over time through diary studies.

How do you research something this emotionally sensitive? How do you observe grief without being intrusive — and how do you translate what you find into design decisions?

Research Approach

Focus Groups

Built trust with bereaved communities before deeper research. Mapped the emotional landscape and identified which aspects of virtual ceremonies mattered most to different cultural groups.

In-depth Interviews

Conducted across five faith and cultural communities to understand how platform affordances shaped the mourning experience differently for each group.

Diary Studies

Tracked how participants' reflections on virtual ceremonies evolved over time, capturing shifts in perspective that single-point interviews would miss.

Video Observation & Heuristic Evaluation

Analysed recorded virtual ceremonies to identify interaction breakdowns, and conducted heuristic evaluations of platform features against the needs of ceremonial use.

What I Found

Users didn't want more flexibility — they wanted structure

Platforms offered open-ended tools (screen share, breakout rooms, chat). But mourners needed guided flows — a clear beginning, middle, and end — that removed decision-making burden during an already overwhelming moment.

Presence mattered more than features

Participants consistently described the most meaningful virtual ceremonies as ones where they could sense other people's presence, even through a screen. Gallery view, ambient audio from attendees, and visible reactions created a sense of shared space that speaker-view and muted-by-default settings actively undermined.

Cultural specificity shaped platform friction

What counted as a "good" virtual ceremony varied dramatically across communities. Platform design choices that worked for one cultural context created friction for another — a universal interface was the wrong frame for this problem.
