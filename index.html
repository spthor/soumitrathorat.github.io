I grew up watching the internet reshape how people connect. In the span of a single childhood, I watched landlines disappear, smartphones become ubiquitous, and social platforms become the default infrastructure for keeping in touch. That shift fascinated me. Not the technology itself, but the human side of it: how different people in different contexts actually used these tools, what they gained, what they lost, and what got hidden in the gaps between what designers intended and what people actually did.

That curiosity became my research practice. I study how different stakeholders in a setting use technology to collaborate, communicate, and maintain relationships. In Pune, I watched an 80-year-old widow teach herself English by reading WhatsApp messages so she could stay connected to her grandchildren. In Galesburg, Illinois, I found that a public-private redevelopment initiative used community narratives to decide who got resources and who got left out. During COVID, I studied how people repurposed video meeting platforms to hold funerals and memorials, discovering that when stakes are high, people want less flexibility and more structure. In rural India, I followed students and teachers as they navigated digital learning tools that measured what was easy to quantify and missed what actually mattered.

The thread connecting these projects is consistent: I'm interested in understanding how the structure of a system shapes behaviour, whose perspectives get amplified and whose get erased, and what happens in the gap between official intent and lived experience. I use ethnographic methods because they let me see not just what people say about their lives, but how they actually navigate complexity, maintain relationships across difference, and make meaning out of the tools available to them.

Outside of research, I play Carnatic classical music on electric mandolin. I listen obsessively to Hindustani classical music and spend too much time with mid-twentieth century satire. I've co-written and directed three plays. I occasionally game. All of these are about the same thing: narrative, performance, how meaning gets constructed in a given context, and how people communicate across difference. Theatre taught me that you can say the same line to different audiences and mean something completely different depending on what they bring to it. That lesson runs through all my work.
